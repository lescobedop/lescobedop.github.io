At some point the question changes. Not where can I go next, but how much time is left.

I am returning to Peru.

Not because what came before has run its course. The years abroad gave back more than I put in, and in directions I could not have predicted. But some seasons cannot be postponed. And I had been postponing.

To live far from family is to live with subtraction. Every reunion is a gift and every farewell carries its own weight. You begin to calculate differently. Not in years remaining, but in ordinary Sundays, dinners without occasion, the mornings your parents move a little slower and you are in a different time zone. Time with loved ones does not accumulate. It evaporates.

Coming back to my parents means leaving temporarily my own young family. My wife understands this without being asked to. She is the kind of person who sees clearly what love requires, not only what it feels like. That clarity is its own kind of strength.

Peru is not a step back. It is a step inward.

There is work to do there. Not for myself, or not only. For the place. For what can still be built. My son is woven into that purpose, though he does not know it yet. What I build there is partly for him, an answer to a question he will ask later about where we come from and what we chose to do with that.

Legacy is a word I use carefully. It is the layering of action and meaning, passed deliberately from one person to the next. The Wolf’s Den is not a place. It moves. And right now, it is heading south.
